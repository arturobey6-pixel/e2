Cast Iron Without the Cult: Care, Cooking and the Myths That Won't Die

No kitchen object attracts mythology like cast iron. The internet will tell you soap destroys it, water murders it, and your grandmother's skillet contains irreplaceable century-old magic. Almost none of this survives contact with chemistry. Here's the demystified version we teach — cast iron as what it actually is: the best $30 pan you'll ever own, asking for ninety seconds of honest care.

What seasoning actually is

Seasoning isn't flavor and isn't magic — it's polymerized oil: thin layers of fat that heat has hardened into a slick, bonded plastic-like coating. Every time you cook with fat in the pan, you're adding microscopically to it. That's the whole secret: seasoning is built by using the pan, not by rituals. A new pan seared and roasted in weekly becomes beautifully non-stick in a couple of months, ritual-free.

The soap myth, retired

The rule dates from when soap contained lye, which genuinely stripped seasoning. Modern dish soap is gentle detergent and does nothing to polymerized oil — wash your cast iron with soap. What actually damages seasoning: soaking overnight (rust), the dishwasher (rust, always), and scrubbing with nothing but rage. A soapy sponge and thirty seconds is correct care, whatever the forum grandpas say.

The 90-second routine

  1. While the pan is still warm, rinse and wipe with a soapy sponge. Stuck bits? Coarse salt and a paper towel, or a minute of simmered water.
  2. Dry completely — towel first, then thirty seconds back on a low burner. Water is the only real enemy in this story.
  3. While warm, rub in half a teaspoon of neutral oil with a paper towel until the pan looks matte, not greasy. Done.

That's the entire religion. Miss a step occasionally; the pan forgives.

Rust rescue (it's never dead)

Orange spots are cosmetic, not fatal. Scrub the rust off with steel wool down to grey metal, wash, dry on the burner, then oven-season once: thin coat of oil all over, upside-down in a 230°C/450°F oven for an hour. One cycle restores a flea-market disaster; there is no such thing as a ruined cast iron pan, only an abandoned one.

What cast iron is actually best at

Its superpower is heat retention — the pan stays screaming hot when food hits it. That means: steaks and chops with real crust, smash burgers, cornbread and deep golden roast potatoes, pan pizza, and oven-to-table anything (the handle survives what would kill other pans). Where it's the wrong tool: delicate fish and eggs before your seasoning matures, anything long-simmered and very acidic (hours of tomato sauce will taste tinny and eat seasoning — use enameled or stainless), and boiling water (rust bath).

Buying advice in one line

A new $30–40 workhorse from any reputable maker cooks identically to the $200 heritage brand once seasoned — buy the cheap one, spend the difference on good steaks to break it in. Vintage pans are lovely (lighter, smoother) if one crosses your path, but they're a pleasure, not a requirement.

We cook on cast iron constantly in class — it's half the searing curriculum in The Weeknight Chef, where you'll feel the heat-retention difference in your own hands. The pan is humble, the results aren't, and now the mythology's out of the way.
