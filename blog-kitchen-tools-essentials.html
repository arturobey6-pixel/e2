The Only 12 Kitchen Tools You Actually Need (From a Teaching Kitchen)

Our teaching kitchen runs over five thousand students a year, and every station carries the same twelve tools. Not because we're minimalists — because after eight years, everything else proved optional. If you're outfitting a kitchen (or wondering which wedding-registry gadgets to quietly return), here's the honest list, with real prices and the reasoning.

The cutting corner (3 tools)

  1. 8-inch chef's knife ($40–120): the tool you'll touch most in your life. One good one, kept sharp, outperforms any block set. (Full guidance in our knife skills guide.)
  2. Big cutting board ($25–50): bigger than feels necessary — cramped boards slow everything and cause accidents. Wood or thick plastic.
  3. Knife sharpener ($15–35): the most under-owned tool in home cooking. A humble pull-through used monthly changes your daily experience more than any pan.

The heat section (4 tools)

  1. 12-inch stainless skillet ($40–90): the everyday pan — searing, sautéing, pan sauces. Stainless, because fond (the browned stuck bits) is flavor, not failure.
  2. Cast iron skillet ($30–40): the heat-retention specialist for steaks, burgers and oven work. (See our cast-iron guide for the no-cult care routine.)
  3. Heavy pot / Dutch oven ($50–150): braises, stews, pasta water, big-batch everything. Enameled if the budget allows; a heavy stockpot if not.
  4. Two sheet pans ($20 for both): the most versatile cheap objects in any kitchen — roasting, sheet-pan dinners, batch browning, cookie duty. Buy the sturdy restaurant-supply kind.

The hands (5 tools)

  1. Digital instant-read thermometer ($15–35): ends guessing forever. Meat, bread, oil, custards — the difference between hoping and knowing. If we could hand every home cook one object, this is it.
  2. Fish spatula ($12): misnamed — it's the best all-purpose flipper for everything, thin and flexible where it counts.
  3. Locking tongs ($12): your heat-proof hand extension. Get 12-inch.
  4. Microplane ($15): garlic, citrus zest, parmesan, ginger, nutmeg — five ingredients that go from chore to seconds.
  5. Kitchen scale ($20): baking becomes reliable, portions become honest, and half your measuring cups retire.

Total for all twelve at the budget end: about $300 — less than one fancy stand mixer.

What didn't make the list (and why)

The garlic press (microplane does it better, cleans easier), single-purpose gadgets — avocado slicers, strawberry hullers, egg separators — that each do one thing a knife and thirty seconds already do, the knife block set (you're paying for five knives you'll never sharpen), and most countertop appliances bought aspirationally. Honorable exceptions once you cook often: a blender or stick blender (soups), a food processor (if you batch-cook), and a stand mixer (if you actually bake weekly — the world's most expensive bread-box otherwise).

The upgrade order

Starting from zero, buy in this order: knife → board → skillet → sheet pans → thermometer → the rest as cooking demands them. Each purchase should be pulled by a real need from last week's cooking, not pushed by a sale. Tools follow cooking; they never create it.

Every one of these twelve lives at every station in our studio — come put your hands on them in any class and feel what right-sized equipment does for confidence. The gadget drawer stays home; the skills come with you.
